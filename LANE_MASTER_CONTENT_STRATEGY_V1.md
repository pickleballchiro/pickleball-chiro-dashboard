## 1a. THE CORE SELLING PHILOSOPHY ⭐ READ THIS BEFORE BUILDING ANY CONTENT

> This section governs every piece of content Lane creates. It supersedes any instruction elsewhere that conflicts with it.

### The Single Most Important Insight

**"You've been taught to educate instead of sell decisions — and that's why content gets likes but no bookings."**

When Lane explains the full mechanism in free content — why the elbow hurts, exactly what's happening upstream in the swing chain, the specific exercises to fix it — Dave gets the dopamine hit of finally understanding. The curiosity loop closes. He feels informed. He moves on. He has no reason to DM Lane, no reason to buy the guide, no reason to book a session. Lane already gave him what he came for.

**Content does not educate. Content mirrors, validates, and opens loops that only the guide or a session closes.**

This is the central error every sports-medicine creator makes: they lead with their clinical knowledge because it feels like proof of value. It is proof of value. But proof of value given away completely is a reason NOT to pay. The expertise is demonstrated by HOW PRECISELY Lane understands Dave's situation — not by explaining the mechanism in a caption.

### The Four-Beat Emotional Arc

Every reel, every carousel, every caption moves Dave through this exact sequence. In this order. Every time.

**1. RECOGNITION — "That's exactly me."**
Name Dave's specific experience with precision. Not "knee pain" — "the knee that feels fine in game one, starts talking in game three, and you spend the drive home wondering how many sessions you have left before it becomes a real problem." The more specific, the stronger the recognition.

**2. VALIDATION — "It's not because I'm old."**
Name why Dave's attempts failed — without blaming Dave for trying them. "You iced it. You rested it. You backed off for two weeks. It came back the first day you played full. That's not a willpower problem. That's a root-cause problem."

**3. THE GAP — "There's something I don't know yet."**
Hint that a specific, fixable cause exists that nobody in Dave's world has identified. Name that it exists. Never explain it.
- ✅ "The reason your knee keeps coming back has nothing to do with the knee."
- ❌ "Your knee pain is caused by poor hip mobility and a faulty split-step pattern that overloads the medial compartment on lateral push-off."

The first creates desire. The second closes the loop. Only one of these sells guides or sessions.

**4. DECISION — "This is where I get it."**
Make commenting KNEE or DMing Lane feel like an act of self-respect — the move of a serious player who is done managing symptoms and ready to actually fix the thing.

**The one test:** After reading this piece of content, does Dave feel *understood and curious* — or *educated and satisfied?* Understood and curious books sessions. Educated and satisfied scrolls.

### What NEVER Goes in Free Content

These are completed answers. They belong inside the paid guide or a booked session only.

- The specific upstream joint causing the knee issue (hip, ankle, thoracic)
- The exact movement pattern causing overload (faulty split step, lateral push mechanics)
- Named exercises or protocols with sets/reps
- Specific swing mechanics corrections that eliminate elbow pain
- Any "here's what to do" numbered sequence
- Any diagnosis of a specific shot type causing a specific injury
- The T-spine / shoulder connection explained in full
- The exact recovery protocol between sessions
- Specific paddle or grip recommendations tied to injury mechanism

**The rule:** If it answers "what do I do" or "what exactly is wrong" → it lives in the guide or the session. If it answers "why this keeps happening and why what I tried didn't work" → it can live in content, but only partially. Name the category of cause. Never complete the explanation.

### The Snippet Strategy

Lane's knowledge covers multiple domains: swing mechanics, body chain, movement patterns, recovery, paddle science, strength and conditioning. These are breadcrumbs, not lessons.

Each snippet does three things:
1. Names a gap Dave didn't know existed ("the reason icing delays recovery, not accelerates it")
2. Validates that the gap is real ("I see this in the clinic every week")
3. Points toward the guide or session as the place where the gap gets filled ("DM me KNEE and I'll walk you through the three questions I ask every player before I touch their knee")

The snippet strategy means no single piece of free content ever completes the picture. Each post is a piece of a puzzle that only assembles inside the guide or on the court with Lane.

### Content Sells Decisions, Not Information

Dave does not need more information. Dave has Googled knee pain. Dave has watched YouTube. Dave has done a round of PT. Dave does not have a knowledge deficit — Dave has a *solution deficit.* They haven't found the right solution yet, not the right information.

Lane's content is not a resource center. It is a decision engine. The decision is: "This person gets my situation better than anyone I've encountered. I want to work with them / buy their guide."

Every word in every post is either moving Dave toward that decision or not. There is no neutral content.

## 4. THE INSTAGRAM ALGORITHM — WHAT ACTUALLY MATTERS

### How the Algorithm Works Right Now

Instagram shows every post to a small test batch first — typically existing followers. If that batch engages (watches fully, comments, shares, saves), Instagram pushes it to a broader non-follower audience. If the test batch scrolls past, the post dies.

**The single biggest implication:** Hook quality is the primary growth lever. A weak hook on a strong piece of content kills it before anyone outside your existing audience ever sees it. One strong Reel with a hook that stops the scroll outperforms 20 weak Reels posted at perfect times.

### Signals That Matter (In Order)

1. **Watch time / completion rate** — Did they watch the whole thing? Partial watches count for less.
2. **Shares** — Someone DM'd it to a friend or reposted it. This is the highest-trust signal.
3. **Saves** — Someone bookmarked it to come back. Carousels designed to be reference material earn saves.
4. **Comments** — Real comments (not emoji-only) signal resonance.
5. **Profile visits after watching** — The algorithm reads this as "this post made them want to know more."
6. **Follows from non-followers** — The growth signal.

### What the Algorithm Punishes

- Low watch time / immediate scroll-away → post gets buried
- Inconsistent posting → momentum resets
- Reposts of other people's content without high engagement → dilutes account signal
- TikTok watermarks → explicitly suppressed by Instagram
- Hashtag spam → no longer a growth lever, now signals spam behavior
- Posting volume without hook quality → trains the algorithm that your account's test batches don't engage

### The Volume vs. Quality Rule

High-frequency posting of weak hooks signals spam behavior to the algorithm. The algorithm treats each post as a test batch. If most tests fail (low watch time, low engagement), Instagram learns to show your content to fewer people. One strong reel with a hook that earns shares and full watches outperforms 50 weak reels. **Done is better than perfect only applies to content with a good hook. A bad hook perfectly produced is still a bad hook.**

### Trial Reels — Use This Feature

Instagram's Trial Reels feature allows a post to be tested with non-followers before going to your main feed. Use this for:
- Testing a new hook angle before committing to a full posting slot
- Validating a topic before building a carousel around it
- Finding which pillar breaks through to new audiences fastest
- Testing Paragraph Overlay vs. Two-Line Hook on the same topic

If a trial reel shows strong non-follower reach — promote it to the main feed. If it doesn't — use the data. The hook failed. Diagnose why and adjust.

**Important:** Trial Reels benchmark separately from regular Reels — they typically underperform because they lack the engagement boost from existing followers. Only compare Trial vs. Trial performance.

### Google Indexing — New in 2026

Public Reels are now indexed by Google and Bing, creating an additional discovery channel beyond Instagram. Optimize by embedding keywords naturally in captions ("pickleball knee pain," "pickleball injury"), using subtitles (auto-indexed as text), and using alt text when available. A reel about "pickleball knee pain" can now surface in Google search results — treat captions as light SEO assets as well as conversion copy.

### Carousels — Production Spec

- **Slides:** 5 maximum. Never more.
- **Size:** 1080×1350px portrait
- **Music:** Add natively in Instagram after posting — pushes carousels into Reels feed
- **Each slide:** One dominant phrase (Oswald Bold headline) + minimal supporting text (Montserrat). Never paragraphs.

1. Cover / Hook — "Is this for me?" answered in 2 seconds
2. Pain — Dave's exact experience named precisely. They should feel it, not read it.

## 6. HOOK LIBRARY & HOOK WRITING RULES

### What a Hook Has to Do

One job: make Dave feel like scrolling past would cost them something. Not "make them curious." Not "introduce Lane." Make them feel that stopping is in their interest. That feeling is created in under 3 seconds.

Every hook answers two questions Dave is unconsciously asking as they scroll:
1. "Is this for me?" — They must self-identify immediately. Not "this might be relevant" — "this is describing my exact situation."
2. "What do I get if I keep watching?" — There must be a payoff. Pain addressed, curiosity resolved, validation they need.

If the hook doesn't answer both in the first 3 seconds, it has failed — regardless of how good the rest of the content is.

### The Three Hook Failure Modes

| Failure Mode | What It Looks Like + Fix |
|---|---|
| **Too long** | The hook is a full sentence when it should be a phrase. Dave's thumb is already moving. Fix: cut everything until only the essential tension remains. |
| **Too logical** | The hook explains instead of interrupts. It sounds like the opening of an article. Fix: hit the emotion first. A description of Dave's exact pain lands before any explanation of it. |
| **Too generic** | "This will help your pickleball game" could be for anyone. When a hook is for everyone, it's for no one. Fix: be specific to the shot type, the injury, the exact situation. |

### The 6 Hook Mechanisms

**Hook Type 1: Named Pain Hook**
Name Dave's exact experience with precision. The more specific, the stronger the response — the physical feeling of being understood.
- Template: "[Specific symptom] that shows up [specific condition or timing]."
- "The elbow pain that only shows up on your backhand drive."
- "Your knee aches after the third game but feels fine in the morning."
- "That shoulder click that started after you started hitting more overheads."
- "The pop in your reset that won't go away no matter what you adjust."

**Hook Type 2: Counterintuitive Claim Hook**
Make a claim that sounds wrong. Dave's brain stops because the pattern is broken. Then deliver the logical explanation. This format performs at 3-5x baseline engagement.
- Template: "[Thing they believe is true] is actually making [the problem] worse."
- "Icing your pickleball elbow is making the recovery slower."
- "Stretching your hamstrings before playing is hurting your court movement."
- "The stronger your forehand, the higher your injury risk — unless you fix this first."
- "Playing through it isn't toughness. It's why you're still dealing with this six months later."

**Hook Type 3: Belief Violation Hook**
Challenge the thing Dave has been told — or the solution they've been trying — without dismissing them for trying it. Highest-trust hook category because it validates frustration before offering an alternative.
- Template: "[What they've been told/tried] isn't wrong. It's just not addressing [the real issue]."
- "Rest didn't fix your knee because rest wasn't the problem. This is."
- "Your PT gave you the right exercises for the wrong joint."
- "Every coach you've seen gave you advice for the shot. Nobody addressed what your body is doing underneath it."
- "Generic sports chiropractors treat the body. They don't watch you play. That's the difference."

**Hook Type 4: Curiosity Gap Hook**
Open a loop that Dave's brain needs to close. The incomplete thought creates mild psychological tension that compels them to keep watching.
- Approach A: Two complete punchy phrases that work together as a pair.
  - "Plays 5 days a week. Can't figure out why the knee keeps coming back."
  - "4.9 DUPR. Sees exactly what's breaking your game down."
  - "Great third shot. Terrible recovery. Same root cause."
- Approach B: One sentence broken at a tension point.
  - "Your knee pain has nothing" / "to do with your knee."
  - "The reason your reset pops up has nothing" / "to do with your grip."
  - "Your elbow isn't the problem." / "Your swing is."

**Hook Type 5: Identity Validation Hook**
Speak to Dave's identity, not their symptom. Content that addresses who they are (a serious player who refuses to slow down) outperforms content that addresses what they have (a knee injury). This touches the core wound.
- Template: "To the player who [describes their exact situation or decision]."
- "To the player managing a nagging injury so they don't have to miss games."
- "To the 55-year-old who plays 5 days a week and refuses to slow down."
- "To the player who's been told to rest and has absolutely no intention of doing that."
- "To everyone who's been to a chiropractor who didn't know what a third shot was."

**Hook Type 6: Credential-by-Proof Hook**
Never lead with credentials ("As a Doctor of Chiropractic..."). Demonstrate understanding so specific that the credential becomes obvious. The knowledge proves the expertise — the title doesn't.
- Template: "I'm a [credential] and here's what I see [specific observation from real experience]."
- "I'm a 5.0 DUPR chiropractor and the most common thing I see is players with great technique and broken bodies."
- "I've played 500+ hours of competitive pickleball and adjusted 100+ pickleball players. Here's what connects almost every injury to a movement pattern."
- "I see both the court and the clinic. The thing coaches miss and doctors miss is the same thing."

### Buzzword Hook Strategy — Front-Load in First 3–5 Words

These words stop Dave's thumb because they've been talking about these topics in their group chat this week.

| Tier 1 — Lead with these | Tier 2 — Reinforce with these |
|---|---|
| pickleball elbow | inflammation |
| knee pain | recovery |
| shoulder pain | footwork |
| playing through pain | split step |
| pickleball at 50/55/60 | movement mechanics |
| injury prevention | overhead mechanics |
| 4.0 / 4.5 player | court movement |
| chiropractor for pickleball | warm-up |
| third shot drop | rating plateau |

### Hook Language to Avoid (Dave's Burn Cycle)

Dave has tried things that didn't work. His skepticism is finely tuned. These phrases trigger it immediately.

| Avoid | Why |
|---|---|
| "Fix your pickleball elbow for good" | "For good" has been promised before. |
| "The secret to..." | There are no secrets. Dave knows this. |
| "Game-changing" | Hype language triggers dismissal instantly. |
| "What nobody tells you" | Every supplement brand and YouTube channel used this. Burned. |
| "Simple fix for..." | Nothing about persistent injuries feels simple to Dave. |
| "For players over 50" (alone) | True but not specific enough. Always pair age reference with a precise situation. |
| "As a Doctor of Chiropractic..." | Leading with the title, not the evidence. Rewrite to demonstrate, not declare. |

### Proven Hook Templates for Lane's Niche

- "Your [body part] pain has nothing to do with your [body part]."
- "The [thing they're doing] is why it keeps coming back."
- "To the player who [exact situation]."
- "Every coach sees [X]. I see [X] and [Y]."
- "Icing it. Resting it. It came back anyway."
- "Your PT gave you the right exercises for the wrong problem."
- "Playing [X] days a week. [Body part] keeps talking."
- "3.5 technique. 4.5 body falling apart underneath it."
- "The [shot] everyone says to fix. Nobody tells you [the body piece]."
- "You don't need to stop playing. You need to stop [specific wrong thing]."
- "Every chiropractor I've been to didn't know what a [shot type] was. Mine does."
- "I've adjusted [X] pickleball players. The same thing connects almost every injury."

### Hook Writing Process — Do This Every Time

1. Generate 8–10 raw hook candidates across different emotional angles and hook types
2. Gut-check each one as Dave: does this describe my exact situation?
3. Kill any hook that could work for a different sport or a different athlete — too generic
4. Kill any hook that completes the loop instead of opening it
5. Keep 1–2 strongest. Develop only those.
6. Write Line 1 (4–7 words on screen) and Line 2 (the twist or gap). Test reading them aloud.

## 7. CAPTION FORMULA

### Caption Structure

| **The Gap** | The answer exists. Point at it. Never complete it. Create desire, not satisfaction. | 1 sentence |
| **CTA** | DM / comment / follow prompt. Specific. Access framing, not ad framing. | 1 line |
| **Engagement Question** | Something Dave actually has an answer to. Drives comments. Feeds the algorithm. | 1 question |

### Caption Rules

- **Short lines only.** One idea per line. No paragraphs. Dave reads in a vertical scroll — walls of text get skipped.
- **Validate before educate.** He needs to feel seen before he'll read anything else.
- **The Tease-Not-Teach Rule:** The caption opens the gap. The DM conversation or guide closes it. If Dave can solve their problem from the caption alone — rewrite it.
- **End with a real question** Dave has an answer to — this drives comments which feed the algorithm.
- **Never complete the loop.** If Dave reads the caption and decides NOT to book or comment — could they still go fix the problem themselves? If yes — rewrite it.
- **No bullet points in caption copy.** Line breaks only.
- **No price mentions in captions or DMs.** Price lives on the Stan Store page.
- **CTA keyword always in ALL CAPS** in captions (Comment KNEE, DM ELBOW, etc.)

### The Hormozi Invisible-Influence Principle ⭐ READ BEFORE WRITING ANY REEL

> The highest level of influence is when the person believes the decision to buy was entirely their own.

Dave does not feel sold to when he feels understood first. Lane's job is not to convince — it is to create the conditions where Dave convinces himself. When the hook is precise enough and the loop stays open, Dave arrives at "Comment KNEE" as his own conclusion — not a response to a pitch.

**The four conditions that make buying feel like Dave's idea:**

**1. Recognition — "He knows exactly what I'm going through."**
The hook names Dave's specific experience so precisely he says "how did he know?" Not "knee pain" — "the knee that's fine in game one, starts talking in game three, and has you Googling how long inflammation lasts on the drive home."

**2. Validation — "It wasn't my fault for trying what I tried."**
Name every solution Dave already tried — and explain why it didn't work — without blaming him for trying it. "You iced it. You rested it. It came back the first day you played full. That's not a discipline problem. That's a root-cause problem." When his failed attempts are validated, he doesn't feel like he failed — he feels like he was missing information. And missing information can be fixed.

**3. The Gap — "There's something specific I don't know yet."**
Name that a specific, fixable cause exists that nobody in Dave's world has identified. NEVER explain it. Name only that it exists.
- ✅ "The reason your knee keeps coming back has nothing to do with the knee."
- ❌ "Your knee pain is caused by poor hip mobility and a faulty split-step pattern that overloads the medial compartment." (Loop closed. No reason to comment.)

**4. The Decision — "This is the move a serious player makes."**
The CTA is never "buy my thing." It's access. It's what a player who takes their body seriously does.
- ✅ "Comment KNEE and I'll send you the guide I walk every player through."
- ❌ "Click the link in my bio to buy my $17 knee guide."

**The decision architecture test — run before finalizing any reel:**
- [ ] Does the hook describe Dave's experience so precisely he'd say "how did he know?"
- [ ] Are Dave's past solutions named without blaming him for trying them?
- [ ] Is the gap real and named — but never explained?
- [ ] Does the CTA feel like a player identity move, not a purchase prompt?
- [ ] After watching — does Dave feel *understood and curious*, or *educated and satisfied*?

Understood and curious → he comments. Educated and satisfied → he scrolls.

### The 3-Second Hold Rate — Algorithm Priority #1

Up to 50% of viewers drop off in the first 3 seconds. The algorithm interprets early drop-off as a signal to stop promoting. Reels with a 3-second hold rate above 60% outperform those below 40% by **5–10x in total reach.**

**The hook is not just important — it IS the algorithm optimization.** The first line of overlay text or the first spoken sentence must stop the scroll before audio even registers. If it fails, the rest of the reel is irrelevant.

**Hook requirements:**
- Appears/is spoken within 1.5 seconds of video start
- Works for sound-off viewers (text overlay, not audio-dependent)
- Passes the "Is this exactly me?" test for Dave — not generic, not broad

---

### Algorithm Signals — In Order of Weight (2026)

1. **Completion rate** — Did they watch the whole thing? Most heavily weighted.
2. **Replays** — Watched it twice = high-value signal.
3. **Shares** — Sent to a friend = highest trust signal in the algorithm.
4. **Saves** — Bookmarked = one save weighs ~10 likes.
5. **Comments** — Especially keyword comments (double signal: algorithm + ManyChat trigger fires).
6. **Likes** — Weakest signal. Don't optimize for these.

**Structure every reel to earn signals 1–5. Likes follow — don't chase them.**

### B-Roll Reel Production Format

**Visual setting:** Real court gameplay, adjustment demos, warmup footage, slow-motion shot breakdowns. Court footage is gold — always preferred over studio or clinic. Real > polished. Authenticity signals credibility in this niche.

**Two primary B-Roll sub-formats:**

#### SUB-FORMAT A: Two-Line Hook (Default — 80% of B-Roll)

This is the standard format. Fast to produce, high algorithmic reach.

- **Line 1 (Hook):** 4–7 words. Oswald Bold. Appears in first 1–2 seconds before audio registers. Must stop the scroll on its own.
- **Line 2 (Gap-widener):** 7–12 words. Appears 2–4 seconds after Line 1. Deepens curiosity or directs to caption. NEVER answers the hook.
- **Caption** does the heavy lifting — emotional mirror + full gap statement + keyword CTA.

**Two-Line Hook Examples:**

✅ Good:
```
Line 1: Your knee is fine until game three
Line 2: That pattern means something. Read the caption.
```
✅ Good:
```
Line 1: Icing doesn't fix pickleball knee pain
Line 2: Here's why it keeps coming back — caption below
```
✅ Good:
```
Line 1: Stop blaming your knee for your knee pain
Line 2: Comment KNEE — I'll explain what I actually look at
```
❌ Bad (explains mechanism):
```
Line 1: Your knee hurts because your hip is weak
Line 2: Fix your glutes and the pain goes away
```
❌ Bad (too generic):
```
Line 1: Knee pain is common in pickleball
Line 2: Here's how to fix it
```

#### SUB-FORMAT B: Paragraph Overlay (Use Sparingly — ~20% of B-Roll)

A single text block of 3–5 sentences. Works when the insight is surprising enough that reading = watching — the act of reading IS the watch time. Best for counterintuitive truths, clinic observations, and belief violations.

**Paragraph overlay rules:**
- Opening sentence hooks in ≤10 words — or readers scroll before finishing
- Each sentence creates forward momentum — ends on tension or an unresolved statement
- NEVER exceed 5 sentences. If 3 sentences do the job — use 3.
- Ends on The Gap. Never on The Answer.

**Paragraph Overlay Example:**
✅ Good:
```
Most pickleball knee pain isn't a knee problem.

You rested it. You iced it. You backed off for two weeks and it came back the first time you played full.

That's not a discipline problem. That's a root-cause problem.

Comment KNEE and I'll show you what I actually look at.
```
❌ Bad (closes the loop):
```
Most pickleball knee pain is caused by how you push off on your split step, which overloads the inside of your knee.

Work on your hip strength and the pain goes away. Try these three exercises.
```

**Universal B-Roll Text Overlay Rules:**
- **Max 4 sentences total** across all overlay text — no exceptions
- **No hyphens** — commas and colons only
- **No clinical/anatomy language** — no named joints, mechanisms, or exercises Dave wouldn't say on the court
- **No named exercises** — ever. Those live in the guide.
- **No complete explanations** — name the category, never the answer
- **CTA keyword always in ALL CAPS** when it appears in overlay
- **Court Orange (#F05A28) accent** on 1–2 key words max per frame — use sparingly
- **White text minimum 40pt** — legible on mobile on dark footage
- **Dark semi-transparent bar** behind text if background is busy

### Talking Head Reel Production Format

Lane speaks directly to camera (or is overlaid with background removed onto court B-roll). Face builds the brand — straight-to-camera builds parasocial trust faster than any other format.

#### Script Format

```
[HOOK — 1 sentence, ≤15 words. First thing Lane says. No preamble. No warm-up.]
[MIRROR — 1–2 sentences. Names Dave's experience precisely — not what's wrong, what he feels.]
[VALIDATION — 1–2 sentences. Names Dave's failed solutions without blaming him for trying them.]
[THE GAP — 1 sentence. A specific, fixable cause exists. Named but never explained.]
[CTA — 1 sentence. "Comment KEYWORD" framed as a player identity move.]
```

**Script length:** 75–115 words. 30–45 seconds at natural speaking pace. **Shorter is always better. 30 seconds is the ideal. 45 seconds is the ceiling. Never go over.**

If the script is running long — cut the VALIDATION beat first, never the Hook or the Gap.

#### The Hook (First Sentence — Non-Negotiable)

The hook IS the first thing Lane says. No "hey guys." No "so today I want to talk about." The hook starts mid-thought.

**Two strongest Talking Head hook types:**

**Call-Out Hook** — Names Dave's exact situation:
- "If your knee feels fine in game one and starts talking by game three — watch this."
- "If you've taken time off, iced it, done PT, and it still comes back — this is for you."
- "Every pickleball player with knee pain has tried at least three things that didn't work."

**Belief Violation Hook** — Challenges something Dave thinks is true:
- "Icing your knee after pickleball is actually making the problem worse."
- "The reason your knee keeps coming back has nothing to do with the knee."
- "Your PT wasn't wrong — they just weren't looking at the right thing."

**Hook rules:**
- First spoken word within 0.5 seconds of video start — no stalling
- Hook must work WITHOUT visual context — Dave could hear it with headphones in
- Never start with: "So," "Hey," "Today," or "I wanted to talk about"

#### The Mirror (1–2 Sentences)

Name Dave's experience precisely — not what's wrong, but what he FEELS.

✅ "You've played through it for months. You're not sure if it's getting worse or you're just used to it."
❌ "Knee pain is a common complaint among recreational pickleball players over 40."

The mirror should make Dave think "how does this guy know exactly what's happening to me?"

#### The Validation (1–2 Sentences)

Name what Dave tried — and why it made sense to try it — without blaming him.

✅ "You've iced it, rested it, taken a week off. It always comes back the first session you play full. That's not a discipline problem."
✅ "You did what every doctor would tell you to do. And it helped — until you played again."

#### The Gap (1 Sentence — The Most Important Line in the Script)

Name that a specific cause exists that nobody has identified yet. NEVER name it.

✅ "The reason it keeps coming back has nothing to do with the knee itself."
✅ "There's something nobody's looked at yet — and once you understand it, everything else starts to make sense."
❌ "Your hip flexors and glutes aren't doing their job, putting all the load on your knee." (Loop closed. No comment needed.)

The gap sentence should create a specific, palpable feeling of: "There's something I don't know yet. I need to know it."

#### The CTA (Last Sentence)

Direct. One action. Framed as player identity — not a purchase.

✅ "Comment KNEE below and I'll send you the guide I walk every player through."
✅ "Comment KNEE and I'll send you what I look at before I touch anyone's knee."
❌ "Click the link in my bio to buy my $17 knee guide."

#### Text Overlays on Talking Head Reels (Mandatory)

Many viewers watch sound-off. Text is non-negotiable even when Lane is speaking.

- **Header (top of frame, 0–2 sec):** 4–7 words, Oswald Bold, white. Hooks sound-off viewers before they hear Lane. Mirrors or compresses the spoken hook.
- **Bottom CTA (lower third, at CTA moment):** "Comment KNEE" or keyword. Court Orange, Oswald Bold. Stays on screen for final 5+ seconds.
- **Mid-reel text (optional):** If the Gap sentence is particularly strong, a single text line can reinforce it. Use sparingly — Lane's face is the content.
